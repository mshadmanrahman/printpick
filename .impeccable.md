# PrintPick — Design Context

## Target Audience
People actively shopping for 3D printers — from complete beginners to professional engineers. Skews technical (comfortable reading specs), heavy Reddit/YouTube consumers, default dark-mode users. Three distinct mindsets arrive at this site:
1. **The Overwhelmed Beginner**: "There are 200 printers. I just want one that works."
2. **The Upgrading Maker**: "I know what I need — show me the best in my category."
3. **The Comparison Shopper**: "I'm deciding between 2-3 printers — help me choose."

All three share one thing: they've already decided to buy. The site's job is to dissolve decision paralysis and guide them to the right (and slightly better) printer.

## Use Cases
- Self-identifying into a buyer persona (First Printer / Maker / Pro / Resin) from the homepage
- Browsing a curated, tiered catalog within their persona with anchor pricing that nudges upward
- Reading an opinionated, data-rich product page that confirms their purchase intent
- Comparing 2-3 printers side-by-side with clear winner declarations
- Clicking through to Amazon via affiliate CTAs with high confidence

## Brand Personality & Tone
**MKBHD meets Wirecutter.** Confident, opinionated, data-backed. The site already knows which printer you should get — it's just letting you arrive at that conclusion yourself.

Not a neutral comparison tool. Not an affiliate farm with 47 "best" lists. This is editorial authority: "We scored 50+ printers across 5 dimensions. Here's what we'd actually buy."

Tone spectrum: 80% confident/direct, 20% warm/encouraging. Never hedging. Never corporate. Never "it depends" — always "here's the one, and here's why."

**Voice examples:**
- YES: "The one most people should get. Seriously."
- YES: "For $200 more, you get twice the speed and an enclosed chamber. Worth it."
- YES: "You don't need the best printer. You need one that works out of the box."
- NO: "This printer may be suitable for some users depending on their needs."
- NO: "There are many great options in this price range."
- NO: "Consider your requirements before making a purchase."

## Design Inspiration
Primary: **MKBHD's visual language** — studio.mkbhd.com, his YouTube thumbnails, the Panels app aesthetic.

### What to steal from MKBHD
- **Dark as default**: True dark backgrounds, not dark gray. Product images float on darkness
- **Product is the hero**: Minimal UI chrome. The physical object dominates every frame
- **Generous whitespace**: Massive breathing room between sections. Nothing feels cramped
- **High contrast typography**: White on dark, bold weights, clean hierarchy
- **No decorative clutter**: No gradients, no glassmorphism, no glow effects. Just product + type + space
- **Confidence in simplicity**: Every element earns its place or gets cut

### Secondary inspiration
- **RTings.com**: Data density done right. Score breakdowns, comparison tables, trust through transparency
- **Apple product pages**: Anchor pricing psychology, progressive disclosure, "just $X more" nudging
- **The Wirecutter**: "Top Pick" badge authority, editorial voice, decisive recommendations

## Typography Direction
- **Headlines**: **Sora** — geometric, bold, tech-forward. Great at large sizes. Tight letter-spacing (-.02em to -.04em) on display sizes. Weight range: 600-800 for headings
- **Body**: **Outfit** — warm geometric, extremely readable at 14-16px. Weight: 400 regular, 500 medium for emphasis. Slightly wider letter-spacing than headlines (+.01em)
- **Mono/Specs**: **Geist Mono** — for scores, dimensions, prices, spec tables. Already in the project. The "data layer" of the typography system
- **Weight pairing**: Bold contrast. 300 for subtle labels, 700-800 for headlines, 400 for body. Never use the same weight twice in a section
- **Sizing**: Fluid with clamp(). Hero headlines: clamp(2.5rem, 5vw, 4rem). Body: clamp(0.9rem, 1.5vw, 1.1rem)

## Color System
Dark-first. Not "dark mode as an afterthought" — dark is the primary palette.

- **Background**: `oklch(0.13 0.015 250)` — deep navy-black, not pure black. Slightly blue-tinted for depth
- **Surface/Cards**: `oklch(0.17 0.012 250)` — elevated dark, distinguishable from background
- **Surface elevated**: `oklch(0.21 0.01 250)` — for hover states, active cards, modals
- **Text primary**: `oklch(0.95 0.005 250)` — warm off-white, not pure white
- **Text secondary**: `oklch(0.65 0.01 250)` — muted but readable
- **Text tertiary**: `oklch(0.45 0.01 250)` — for captions, metadata
- **Brand/Accent**: Keep the teal `oklch(0.62 0.14 192)` — it pops beautifully on dark backgrounds
- **Brand muted**: `oklch(0.62 0.14 192 / 12%)` — for subtle accent backgrounds
- **Success/Winner**: `oklch(0.65 0.15 145)` — green for "winner" indicators in comparisons
- **Warning**: `oklch(0.75 0.15 80)` — amber for "step up" nudges
- **Borders**: `oklch(0.25 0.01 250)` — subtle, 1px. Never thick borders on dark backgrounds
- **Destructive**: Keep existing `oklch(0.577 0.245 27.325)`

### Color principles
- Tint ALL neutrals toward the blue-250 hue. Even grays should feel cohesive, not flat
- The accent teal is used sparingly — CTAs, badges, active states. Not decoration
- No gradients on text. No glow effects. No neon-on-dark "AI aesthetic"
- Product images provide all the color the page needs. The UI is the frame, not the painting

## Layout Principles
- **Product images dominate**: Printer images are large, high-quality, and given room to breathe. They are not thumbnails stuffed into card corners
- **Asymmetric sections**: Not everything centered. Left-aligned text with right-heavy imagery creates visual interest
- **Varied spacing rhythm**: Tight within groups (specs, badges), generous between sections. Don't use the same padding everywhere
- **No card-in-card nesting**: One level of elevation only. Background → card. Never card → card
- **Grid, not equal columns**: Use CSS grid with intentional sizing. The "Best Value" card is physically larger than the others. Visual hierarchy through size, not just labels
- **Sticky CTAs on mobile**: The "Check Price" button follows scroll. Never more than one thumb-reach away
- **Full-bleed sections**: Hero and tier page headers use full viewport width. Content sections use max-w-6xl

## Motion & Animation
Restrained and purposeful. MKBHD aesthetic = stillness with precise moments of motion.

- **Page load**: Staggered fade-up for sections (transform + opacity, 150ms stagger). One orchestrated entrance, not scattered animations
- **Score bars**: Horizontal fill animation on scroll-into-view. 600ms ease-out-quart. The data "arrives"
- **Card hover**: Subtle lift (translateY -2px) + border color shift to brand teal. 200ms ease-out
- **CTA buttons**: Scale 1.02 on hover, background color transition. Quick, confident
- **Comparison winner**: Brief pulse on the winning score cell when comparison loads
- **No bounce, no elastic, no spring physics**: This is a product review site, not a game. Smooth deceleration only
- **Easing**: `cubic-bezier(0.22, 1, 0.36, 1)` (ease-out-quint) as the global default
- **Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`**: All animations wrapped in media query check

## The Apple Psychology Layer
This is the conversion architecture that makes PrintPick an affiliate revenue machine, not just a review site:

1. **Identity-first entry**: Homepage presents 4 paths (First Printer / Maker / Pro / Resin). Users self-select into a tribe. This creates buy-in before they see a single product
2. **Anchor pricing**: Each tier page shows Budget Pick → Best Value (★ highlighted) → Step Up. The budget option exists to make Best Value feel justified. The Step Up exists to make the upgrade feel small ("just $200 more")
3. **Opinionated recommendations**: Every tier has ONE clear winner with a prominent badge. Not "these are all great." Instead: "This is the one. Here's why."
4. **Loss-framed upgrades**: "For $200 more, you get 2x speed and an enclosed chamber" — frame the delta, not the total price
5. **Comparison as closer**: Product pages end with "How does this compare?" showing 2-3 alternatives. The user never leaves the funnel — they just refine within it
6. **Scarcity of choice**: Don't show all 50 printers at once. Curated tiers of 6-12 printers reduce cognitive load. Fewer options = faster decisions = more clicks

## Anti-Patterns (What NOT to Do)
- NO light mode as primary (dark is default. light mode can exist as a toggle but is not the design target)
- NO gradient text anywhere (the lazy "impact" choice — use font weight and size instead)
- NO glassmorphism, blur effects, or glow borders (decorative, not purposeful)
- NO rounded-everything softness (use sharp corners for cards, selective rounding for buttons/badges only)
- NO equal-sized card grids (visual hierarchy through size difference, not just labels)
- NO "it depends" copy (always make a recommendation)
- NO hero metric layouts (big number + small label template — this is an affiliate site, not a dashboard)
- NO purple-to-blue gradients (the "AI startup" look)
- NO decorative icons above every heading
- NO modals for anything (use inline expansion or page navigation)
- NO stock photography (DALL-E generated product shots only, consistent editorial style)

## Technical Constraints
- Framework: Next.js 16 App Router + React 19
- Styling: Tailwind CSS 4 + shadcn/ui (customized for dark-first)
- Fonts: next/font with Google Fonts (Sora, Outfit) + local (Geist Mono). Zero layout shift
- Images: next/image for all printer photos. DALL-E generated, 1024x1024, editorial product photography style
- Performance: Core Web Vitals green. Animations use transform/opacity only (GPU-composited). Static generation for all printer pages
- Accessibility: WCAG AA minimum. All text passes contrast on dark backgrounds. Focus indicators visible. Screen reader labels on interactive elements
- SEO: Schema.org structured data on all product pages. Metadata on every route. Sitemap auto-generated
- Affiliate: Amazon ASIN-based URLs. CTA buttons must be prominent, accessible, and never hidden below the fold on any viewport
- Data: TypeScript-first. All printer data typed with readonly interfaces. Immutable patterns throughout
